    UNITED NATIONS CONVENTION ON THE LAW OF SEA AND DISPUTES REVOLVING AROUND SOUTH CHINA SEA

    ABSTRACT

    The South China Sea has been one of the most discussed geographical region post WW2 geopolitical scenario. China’s imperialistic ambitions in the region have hamstrung the interest of South East Asian nations leaving them impuissant. The following dispute in the South China Sea revolves around the regions economic value lying in its rich fisheries , crude oil and natural gas importance which entices the European Union , The United States Of America and India.

    KEYWORDS

    UNCLOS, Imperialism, Permanent court of arbitration, Salami Slicing, Cabbage Wrapping, FONOP (Freedom of navigation operations).

    INTRODUCTION

    The sea lies in the south of China hence it is known as South China Sea. The Indian Ocean is connected with South China sea through the Strait of Malacca, which is also known as the world’s busiest trade route. The South China sea is connected with the East China sea through the Farmosa Strait named after the ex-Taiwan (Farmosa Island) which lies in the middle of the strait. The countries which are involved in the South China sea dispute are namely Malaysia, Philippines, Brunei and Vietnam which stand against the claims of China. The basis of China lies in its historical texts. It blatantly refuses to recognise the UNCLOS and the Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) which has made the region a disputed hotspot in geopolitics. The suspected crude oil and natural gas deposits have drawn the attention of western nations towards it. India has also been one of the most active players in the region since China became hostile and a close ally of Pakistan.

    RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

    This paper uses the doctrinal method of research and is structured upon secondary sources of information. It makes use of the various trade agreements, conventions and treaties. The paper also uses data from the official sources of involving governing bodies which are recognised internationally.

    REVIEW OF LITERATURE

    The only way to end the South China sea dispute is that China should abide to the UNCLOS and recognise the EEZ of the neighbouring nations. It should respect their economic interests and the resources which lie in their EEZ. Western nations and India should take strict stand on laws, globally and regionally. The only thing that ASEAN nations can do, should they really want to stand together against China, is to first focus on developing the individual countries’ economies and together gradually try to reduce their dependence on Chinese products, markets, and aid. ASEAN should reduce competition among its members, increase internal aid programs, and exchange technology and experiences. Only when each and every ASEAN nation is strong enough and really enjoys cooperation within the organization, can a common approach finally be considered. But in the age of a prosperous ASEAN, there would no longer be a need to raise this sensitive issue against China. Seeing ASEAN growing stronger economically will entice China to behave moderately — to cooperate rather than take the offensive in the South China Sea. This is the only peaceful solution to the conflict.

    CHINA’S ROAD TO ‘HISTORIC CLAIMS’

    In May 2009, Chinese diplomats attached a map of U-shaped line to an official submission to the United Nations Convention on The Limits of Continental Shelf. It’s meaning as given by Dr.Wu Shieun was as follows:-

    • Sovereignty over the features within this line
    • Sovereignty of rights and jurisdiction over waters as defined by UNCLOS.
    • Historic rights over fishing, navigation and resource developments.

    The problem for the region and the world lies in the third point of the above document. China’s legal scholars are trying hard to justify China’s enjoyment of historic rights to waters upto 1500 kms away from the disputed Chinese territory. According the to pioneering work of a Canadian scholar, the U shaped line was never meant to justify China’s historic claims rather than it was a cartographic device used back then in 1946-47 to indicate which islands did China claimed and which it did not.

    Another, Christopher Chung, a PhD student at the University of Toronto, is the first person to forensically examine the archives of the official Republic of China (ROC) committee that drew the line. In the meeting between the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Ministry of the Interior, Ministry of National Defense, and ROC Navy General Headquarters, it was clearly laid down in the above that the U-shaped line and its meaning was clear to that ROC committee: it defined “the scope of what is to be received for the purpose of receiving each of the islands of the South China Sea”. The committee’s interest was only in the islands. They made no mention of waters, historic or otherwise.

    Nothing changed in China’s claims even after the Communist revolution in 1949. Chinese premier Zhou Enlai denounced the Treaty Of San Francisco 1931. He talked only of the islands not of waters. In 1958, ‘the declaration o territorial sea’ claimed 12 nautical miles of territorial sea- explicitly mentioning that the islands were separated from the mainland by the high seas. Again, we find no mention of historic claims. China’s claims became vague in January 1974. Just before the battle of Paracels in which Chinese forces evicted Vietnamese forces from the western half of those islands and on January 12, people’s daily declared that ‘The resources of these islands and their adjacent seas belonged entirely to China.’ This was not a historic claim either, rather than it was the first sign that China understood the implications of the negotiations of UNCLOS. Which began an year before and claims that maritime resources would be measured from the coast and the islands.

    The PRC participated in all the negotiations that were held till 1982. The final texts of the UNCLOS to which everyone agreed including China was that to make no mention of ‘Historic’ rights. China ratified the convention in 1996. So, the ROC government didn’t claim “historic rights” in the South China Sea in 1946-8 and neither did the PRC in its 1958 decree, its 1974 statement, at the UNCLOS negotiations, or subsequently in its 1992 Law concerning Territorial Waters and Adjacent Regions. However something changed in the late 1990’s however. By the time when PRC passed it’s law on EEZ and the continental shelf in 1998. The officials felt it necessary to include that the ‘provisons of this law shall not affect the historic rights enjoyed by the PRC’. The term historic rights only entered the Chinese lexicon in the late 1990’s.

    The events that gave rise to such a situation are evidently the active persuasion by a American oil company named Crestone which was owned by and individual, Randall C Thompson. He signed a deal with Philippines that open his eyes to the potential riches of the South China Sea. His engineers advised him that next big play would become around the Spratley islands.

    In April 1991, Thompson travelled to South China Sea Institute of oceanography, Guangzhou. There he discovered the richness of resources lying in the South East of Vietnam. Since then he started to persuade higher Chinese authorities with the help of Daniel J. Dzurek, the former chief of boundary division of US department of State. Thompson kept trying to persuade the Chinese to take Crestone seriously until, in February 1992, after much deliberation at the highest levels in Beijing, he finally got to pitch his proposal to the board of the China National Offshore Oil Company (CNOOC). Thompson took along a legal advisor to precisely define the patch of seabed he wanted the rights to. He seems to have accepted – and developed – the idea that China had “historic rights” in the area beyond those spelled out in UNCLOS. Since then PRC, translated the U shaped line into traditional sea boundary line.

    WHAT ARE 9 DASH LINE AND 11 DASH LINE?

    In the year 1947, the PRC introduced the 11 dash line, an unofficial map was released and whole South China Sea was declared a part of it, to mark the area of extent the geographer made the use of 11 dash line. With 11 dashes at the time, it encompassed most of the South China Sea. The Chinese communist party adopted the map in 1949, but removed two dashes to give Gulf Of Tonkin to Vietnam as a courtesy, Within the dashes were key archipelagos- including Spratly and Paracel islands-and various other features, including a set of coral reefs near the Philippines.

    WHY IS CHINA BUILDING ARTIFICIAL ISLANDS?

    China is building artificial islands to find an alternate method which is recognisable by international law according to it. China now claims the extent of area lying within its EEZ by measuring 200 nautical miles from those artificial islands. Moreover it is claimed by China that these islands are not artificially created but were already a part of China since beginning but were submerged due to high seas. Under UNCLOS, such artificial islands don’t have any maritime rights, and nor do submerged reefs. China has built an island with a runway for fighter jets, a hospital with its own garden, and even a farm with about 500 animals. This kind of action is also prompting environmental damage while it is giving China a base for air and sea patrols.

    HOW IS VIETNAM COUNTERING CHINA?

    In order to sustain Indian presence in the hydrocarbon-rich South China Sea, Vietnam has decided to extend the contract for exploration of oil and gas resources in a significant oil block in the region to ONGC Videsh Ltd (OVL). India is also using it as a juncture to checkmate China in the chess table of geopolitics. China is working closely with India’s oldest rival, Pakistan on the CPEC (China Pakistan Economic Corridor) and the following project passes through the PoK (Pakistan Occupied Kashmir) which is a disputed territory. China’s development in the region directly trespasses with India’s internal affairs and it is an indication that China recognises Pakistan as the rightful owner of the disputed territory.

    As Vietnam gave Indian companies the contract to extract natural gas and oil reserves, in return India has given a $50 million defence credit line to Vietnam to purchase military hardware from India to safeguard it coasts against Chinese territories.

    HISTORY OF VIETNAM AND GENEVA ACCORD OF 1954

    The decision to invade Vietnam was made by Napoleon III in July 1857. It was the result not only of missionary propaganda but also, after 1850, of the upsurge of French capitalism, which generated the need for overseas markets and the desire for a larger French share of the Asian territories conquered by the West. The military of the then imperialistic Japan invaded the French controlled Vietnam in September 1940 and remained there until the end of WW2 (1945 August).

    Japan occupies, annexes and renames the Spratly Islands "Shinnan Shoto "(New Southern Islands) on 30th March 1939 and places them under the Governor-General of Formosa. Later, the IJN establishes a seaplane base and a submarine base at Itu Aba (Nagashima -"Long Island") the largest of the Spratly Islands.  On 4th April 1939, Japanese troops invade the Paracels. Japan claims the islands were Chinese territory and that Japan is at war with China. Japan then declares the islands as a Japanese Protectorate. Till the end of WW2 which ended after USA nuked Japan, it constantly used these two islands for constant tactical operations in the region.

    Japan was made to sign the Treaty Of San Francisco in 1951 in which it had to relinquish its control from both the islands. Although it must be noted that the treaty did not specify the new status of these islands. The ASEAN nations were busy in doing negotiations while China made various claims on these islands during the 1951 treaty.

    The deciding factor was the Battle of Dien Bien Phu in 1954, where the French were decisively defeated. This resulted in French withdrawals, and the Geneva conference. It was decided that Vietnam would be divided at the 17th parallel until 1956, when democratic elections would be held under international supervision. After the division of North and South Vietnam, The Paracels and Spratley islands became a part of South Vietnam as it was a part of it’s EEZ. However, two years later North Vietnam claimed that these two islands historically belongs to PRC.

    VIGILATION BY INTERNATIONAL LAW AND UNCLOS

    The Hague based Permanent Court of Arbitration in it’s ruling of October 29th, 2015) said that it has jurisdiction over case filed by Philippines, which contests against China’s claims to nearly 90% of the South China Sea. In response to the aforementioned PCA’s decision, on October 30, 2015, China released the Statement of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the People’s Republic of China in which it mentioned that China will disregard any findings by the PCA on the case brought by the Philippines, saying it is “null and void, and has no binding effect on China. China has its own depositions but the statement has no basis in international law.

    China’s denial of PCA’s jurisdiction over the matter is clear ignorance od Article 288(4) UNCLOS which reads as: “In the event of a dispute as to whether a court or tribunal has jurisdiction, the matter shall be settled by decision of that court or tribunal.” Its worth to mention the Southern Bluefin Tuna Case, in which Australia and New Zealand’s response to the UNCLOS Annex VII Arbitral Tribunal, in which it was stressed that: “These provisions [Part XV of UNCLOS – Settlement of Disputes] clearly imply that a tribunal exercising jurisdiction to determine its jurisdiction under Article 288(4) of UNCLOS should lean in favour of the effectiveness and comprehensive character of the dispute settlement regime, itself a key aspect of the UNCLOS regime. It should not accept arguments which would minimise the effectiveness of Part XV, lending themselves to easy evasion of its provisions.”

    It must be highlighted that both Philippines and China are signatories to the UNCLOS, Philippines ratified the UNCLOS on May 8, 1984 and China on June 7, 1996. According to the Article 287 of the UNCLOS: When there is a dispute arising, both parties must consent to the same procedure for settlement of dispute. However, if there is no agreement on procedures, then the dispute must go to binding arbitration. The arbitration’s award shall be final and without appeal, which shall be complied with by the parties to the dispute. The 1969 Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties contains this rule in Article 26 which provides that “every treaty in force is binding on the parties to it and must be performed by them in good faith.”

    According to the Article 9 of the Annex VII to the UNCLOS expressly addresses the situation in which if one of the members to the dispute refuses to take part in the arbitration process then the failure by a party to defend it’s case shall not bar the proceedings. Which means that China is a part to arbitration and pursuant to the provisions of Article 296(1) UNCLOS and Article 11 of Annex VII and is bound by all the awards given by the tribunal.
