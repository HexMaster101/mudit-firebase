    THE QUESTIONS OF INTERNATIONAL LAW ON ISRAEL-PALESTINE CONFLICT

    ABSTRACT

    The Israel-Palestine conflict, marked by controversy and bloodshed, holds significant interest for international legal scholars worldwide. It encompasses various elements of international law, ranging from statehood to international humanitarian law. The domains of international law covered by the conflict extend beyond a mere dispute, as it is intricately linked with historical, political, and socio-cultural factors. Therefore, it is essential to explore the various questions of international law raised by the conflict, along with the role of various international instruments that shape the concept of 'International Comity.' This exploration ultimately focuses on statehood as an abstract yet complex subject in the domain of Public International Law.

    INTRODUCTION

    The Israel-Palestine conflict has been a long-standing issue since the dawn of the modern International Regime, generating immense interest from various Human Rights groups. Its roots can be traced back to the British Mandate for Palestine and the Balfour Declaration, where both belligerents were promised the formulation of their independent states. The drive for Israeli independence was further intensified by the 'Zionism' movement in the aftermath of World War II. It wasn't until the 1948 UN partition plan that the two-state solution was implemented to address the prolonged conflict, leading to the Israeli War of Independence and subsequent wars. This ongoing saga of wars and conflicts on a socio-religious level raised various questions for the international community.

    SETTLEMENTS IN THE WEST BANK AND EAST JERUSALEM

    One of the most contentious issues in the Israel-Palestine conflict is the construction and expansion of Israeli settlements in the West Bank and East Jerusalem. Many consider these settlements a violation of international law, particularly under the Fourth Geneva Convention. Article 49 of the Fourth Geneva Convention states that the transfer of an occupying power's civilian population into the territory it occupies is a breach of the convention. Even the ICJ Advisory Opinion on Legal Consequences of the Construction of a Wall in the Occupied Palestinian Territory found the construction of the separation barrier in the West Bank to be in violation of international law. United Nations Security Council Resolution 242 (1967) and Resolution 338 (1973) emphasize the inadmissibility of acquiring territory by force

    GAZA BLOCKADE

    Israel's blockade of the Gaza Strip, in place since 2007, has been criticized as a violation of international law. Critics argue that the blockade restricts the movement of people and goods, leading to humanitarian crises. The United Nations Human Rights Council has issued reports and resolutions condemning the blockade as a violation of international law. The Palmer Report (2011), which examined the Gaza flotilla incident, found the blockade to be legal but called for its "easing." United Nations General Assembly Resolution 194 (1948) asserts the right of Palestinian refugees to return to their homes.

    QUESTION OF STATEHOOD

    Two main theories of statehood exist: the declaratory theory and the constitutive theory. The declaratory theory suggests that an entity can attain statehood simply by declaring it. Certain principles that may apply to the theory can be found in various ICJ advisory opinions. For example, the ICJ Advisory opinion on Kosovo mentions that principles of international relations are essentially limited to the sphere of 'International relations.' Thus, one cannot expect compliance with International Humanitarian Law from organizations like the PLO and Hamas, which are abstract entities by nature. The constitutive theory of statehood mandates four criteria, as outlined in the Montevideo Convention, for attainment. The UN partition plan manifested the two-state solution, something also applied to the Indian subcontinent. Through this UN resolution, Israel declared its independence, which led to the first war. However, it's worth noting that even though Israel welcomed this resolution, the Arab world rejected it. Therefore, it would be challenging for the PLO to establish the existence of a Palestinian state, implying the fulfillment of such criteria under the convention. Thus, Palestine's statehood remains a distant prospect, considering the Advisory Opinion on Legal Consequences of the Construction of a Wall in the Occupied Palestinian Territory. The ICJ, while treating Israel as an occupier, attempted to enforce the general principles of laws concerning the occupation of a hostile power in international law, primarily outlined in the 1907 Hague Regulations and the Fourth Geneva Convention, as well as in specific provisions of Additional Protocol I and customary international law. However, following the constitutive theory of statehood, Palestine and the Arab world, which had rejected the UN partition plan, lack the right to claim Palestine as a state. Thus, without statehood, Palestine cannot assert claims of sovereignty and territorial integrity, which are typically matters for international relations between states, as reiterated in the ICJ's Advisory Opinion on Kosovo.

    CONCLUSION

    The Israel-Palestine conflict is a deeply entrenched and multifaceted issue with numerous legal dimensions. Violations of international law in the conflict remain subjects of ongoing debate and contention. Key areas of concern include Israeli settlements in the West Bank and East Jerusalem, the Gaza blockade, the use of force, the rights of Palestinian refugees, and questions regarding the recognition and statehood of Palestine. While international bodies such as the United Nations, the International Court of Justice, and human rights organizations have issued various resolutions and opinions, these matters remain highly contested. The loss of life and dignity, coupled with the growing divide between the people fueled by hatred, is a matter of great concern that can only be resolved when the fundamental elements of this conflict, mainly involving the political and religious enforcement of ideologies, are brought to an end. In contemporary times, institutions like the Oslo and the Abraham Accords are attempting to mitigate the deteriorating situation between the two parties. However, external factors, primarily the religious conflict, draw the attention of the world to this conflict, which must be addressed with consensus. Without this, an appropriate resolution to this conflict remains elusive.
